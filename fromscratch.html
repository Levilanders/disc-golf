The Game

Disc-over the Game

Disc golf is a flying disc sport played much like traditional golf, where players throw a disc from a tee area toward a target — typically a metal basket with hanging chains — in as few throws as possible. Players choose from different disc types — drivers for distance, mid-ranges for control, and putters for short accurate shots — each engineered with specific flight characteristics to shape the perfect throw. Whether you're a casual weekend player or a competitive enthusiast, disc golf offers a unique blend of athleticism, strategy, and nature that keeps players coming back to the course.

The Numbers

Reading a Disc

On each disc, these attributes are measured:

  1. 1
    Speed
    • Ranges from 1 to 14 and measures how fast the disc must travel to achieve its intended flight
    • Higher speed discs cut through the air more efficiently but require more arm speed to fly correctly
    • Beginners should start with lower speed discs (4–6) for better control and accuracy
  2. 2
    Glide
    • Ranges from 1 to 7 and describes the disc's ability to maintain lift during flight
    • Higher glide discs stay in the air longer, which can add distance but makes them harder to control in wind
    • Lower glide discs drop faster and are more predictable in windy conditions
  3. 3
    Turn
    • Ranges from +1 to -5 and represents the disc's tendency to turn right during the high-speed portion of flight (for right-handed backhand throws)
    • A rating of -3 to -5 means the disc will turn significantly to the right, making it more understable
    • A rating of 0 or +1 means the disc resists turning and flies straighter or fades early
  4. 4
    Fade
    • Ranges from 0 to 5 and describes how hard the disc hooks left at the end of its flight (for right-handed backhand throws)
    • Higher fade discs finish with a strong left hook, useful for curving around obstacles
    • Lower fade discs finish straighter and are more beginner friendly
  5. 5
    Stability
    • A combined measure derived from turn and fade that summarizes the overall flight tendency of the disc
    • Overstable discs (positive stability) resist turning and fade hard — reliable in wind and for experienced throwers
    • Understable discs (negative stability) turn easily and are great for newer players or specific shot shapes like rollers and turnover shots
